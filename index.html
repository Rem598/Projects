Left-Digit Bias Audit: Pricing Psychology

Does X.99 pricing actually work? I analyzed 185,000+ transactions to find out.

Finding: It works for some products, not others. Phones showed a +5.16% sales lift with X.99 pricing (p<0.001). Laptops showed no effect at all. Pricing psychology is category-specific, not a universal lever.

Impact: Retailers applying charm pricing uniformly are leaving money on the table in some categories and wasting it in others. This analysis tells you exactly which is which.

Beyond the Treadmill:A Multivariate Analysis of Exercise and Mental Health

Does exercise actually help depression or are we missing the bigger picture in the data?

Finding: Physical activity reduces depression, but explains only 0.25% of the variance in isolation[cite: 11]. A whole-person multivariate model revealed that Age and BMI are actually stronger mathematical predictors than exercise alone. Segmenting the data exposed a "High-Risk" cluster that exercises maximally yet exhibits a 67.5% clinical depression prevalence.

Impact: Demolished the simplistic "exercise equals happiness" narrative by proving depression is a complex, interrelated condition. Statistically demonstrated that forcing continuous behavioral data into categorical buckets destroys the mathematical signal.

Impact: A frequentist test tells you whether the result is significant. This tells you the probability that the decision is correct. This matters for real time business decisions.

Nobel Prize Trends Analysis (1901 to 2025)

How has collaboration in science changed over time? Are researchers working alone or together?

Finding: Solo science is functionally extinct. In the early 1900s, 78% of prizes went to individuals. Today that figure is 35% and still falling. The gender gap is closing too: 6.6% female laureates historically, 19.4% in the 2020s.

Impact: 124 years of data telling a clear structural story about how scientific knowledge gets produced. Collaboration is almost the default.
